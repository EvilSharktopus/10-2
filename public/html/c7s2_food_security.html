The Hunger Timeline
How we got here · Key turning points
📉
Pre-2020
Global hunger had been slowly declining for decades. The world was cautiously optimistic about hitting Zero Hunger by 2030.
🦠
2020–2022
COVID-19 and supply chain collapse push hunger up by 150 million people in two years — the largest increase in recorded history.
⚡
2022–2023
Russia's invasion of Ukraine disrupts global grain supply. Ukraine and Russia together supply 30% of the world's wheat. Food prices spike globally.
📊
2024
Hunger falls by 15 million — but remains above pre-pandemic levels. Africa and Western Asia still rising. Asia and Latin America improving.
🔮
2030
At current pace: 512 million still hungry. UN Zero Hunger goal will be missed. Climate change, conflict, and inequality are the main drivers going forward.

Sources: FAO SOFI 2025; WFP 2025; UN SDG Progress Report.

🌍 Why Africa?
Hunger in Africa is driven by a combination of conflict, climate shocks (drought and flooding), colonial-era land distribution, and debt that prevents governments from investing in food systems. Over 20% of the continent's population now faces hunger — the highest rate of any region.
🔗 The Globalization Connection
Global food systems mean a drought in one country, a war in another, or a shipping disruption anywhere can spike food prices everywhere. Countries that export cash crops for global markets may have hungry people at home. The same system that feeds the wealthy world efficiently can leave the poorest exposed.
💡 What Actually Works
Brazil, Mexico and South American countries reduced hunger using conditional cash transfers and school feeding programs. Southern Asia (led by India) made major gains through investment in domestic food production. Social programs, not just economic growth, drove the improvements.
